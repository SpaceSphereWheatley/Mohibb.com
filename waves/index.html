The physics, end to end

Everything this page computes, term by term — with the equation behind each and where it shows up on screen. Surface elevation is \(\eta\), wavenumber \(k\), angular frequency \(\omega\), water depth \(h\), gravity \(g\), wave height \(H\), phase speed \(c\), group speed \(c_g\).

Linear (Airy) wave theory

Linear theory assumes the wave height is small compared to both the wavelength and the depth, which lets the free-surface boundary conditions be linearized. The payoff is that any sum of sinusoidal solutions is itself a solution — superposition holds. A single linear wave is simply:

$$\eta(x,t) = A\cos(kx - \omega t)$$

The wave group in the top panel is built from several of these, each with a slightly different \(\omega_i\) (and matching \(k_i\)), summed together.

The dispersion relation

Linear theory's boundary-value problem reduces to one equation linking frequency, wavenumber, and depth — the dispersion relation:

$$\omega^2 = gk\tanh(kh)$$

In deep water (\(kh\gg1\), \(\tanh\to1\)) this simplifies to \(\omega^2\approx gk\); in shallow water (\(kh\ll1\), \(\tanh(kh)\approx kh\)) it becomes \(\omega^2\approx ghk^2\), i.e. waves of every frequency travel at the same speed \(\sqrt{gh}\). In between there's no closed form for \(k(\omega)\) — this page solves it numerically with Newton–Raphson, every time the period or depth slider moves.

Phase speed vs. group speed

A single crest travels at the phase speed \(c=\omega/k\). A wave group's envelope travels at the group speed, the derivative of the dispersion relation:

$$c_g=\frac{d\omega}{dk}=\frac{c}{2}\left(1+\frac{2kh}{\sinh(2kh)}\right)$$

In deep water this gives the famous \(c_g=c/2\): individual crests are born at the back of the group, race forward through it, and vanish at the front — exactly what the tracers in the top panel do. In shallow water \(c_g\to c\): every crest and the envelope move together, non-dispersively.

Beyond linear: Stokes second-order

Real waves aren't perfectly sinusoidal: crests are sharper and troughs are flatter than \(\cos\) allows. Stokes expansion adds a second-order correction at twice the wavenumber and frequency:

$$\eta_{\text{Stokes}}=\frac{H}{2}\cos\theta+\frac{\pi H}{8}\cdot\frac{H}{\lambda}\coth(kh)\left[3\coth^2(kh)-1\right]\cos(2\theta)$$

with \(\theta=kx-\omega t\). The correction term scales with steepness \(H/\lambda\) — turn up the wave height in the panels above and watch the Stokes curve in the comparison plot peel away from the Linear curve.

Shallow water: cnoidal waves

In shallow water, nonlinearity reshapes a wave into tall, narrow crests separated by long, flat troughs — the cnoidal wave, built from the Jacobi elliptic function \(\mathrm{cn}\):

$$\eta(x,t)=\eta_{\text{trough}}+H\,\mathrm{cn}^2\!\left(\frac{2K(m)}{\lambda}(x-ct),\,m\right)$$

where \(K(m)\) is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind and \(m\) (computed here from the wave's Ursell number, a steepness-over-depth measure) controls the shape: \(m\to0\) recovers a plain sinusoid, \(m\to1\) approaches an isolated solitary wave. This page maps Ursell number to \(m\) with a simple monotonic approximation tuned for the right qualitative shape, not the full cnoidal/KdV dispersion closure — see the caveats below.

What's simplified

The dispersion solver, Stokes correction, and group-velocity formula are exact linear-theory results. The cnoidal curve's elliptic parameter \(m\) is fit with an approximate Ursell-number mapping rather than solved from the full KdV dispersion relation — good for showing the right trend (sinusoid → peaked crest → solitary wave as steepness/shallowness increases), not for engineering-grade prediction.

Stokes and cnoidal theory each describe a single monochromatic wave train; neither one is valid to simply add together the way linear waves are, which is why the wave-group/superposition demo above stays strictly linear, while the comparison plot below shows all three theories as single waves rather than groups.

The seabed is assumed flat and uniform, the sea is unidirectional (no directional spreading), and there's no wave breaking, wind input, or dissipation modeled anywhere on this page.
